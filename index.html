Overview

A comprehensive breakdown of the first dedicated benchmark for statistical reasoning in LLMs

Core Overview

Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable advances in mathematical and logical reasoning, yet statistics— as a distinct and integrative discipline— remains underexplored in benchmarking efforts. To address this gap, we introduce StatEval, the first comprehensive benchmark dedicated to statistics, spanning both breadth (across subdomains) and depth (across difficulty levels).

StatEval comprises two key components:

  • 13,817 foundational problems: Covering undergraduate and graduate statistics curricula (e.g., probability, statistics, machine learning).
  • 14,816 research-level proof tasks: Extracted from top-tier statistical journals (2020–2025) to assess advanced theoretical reasoning.
This section details StatEval’s core framework, including data classification, extraction pipeline, evaluation methodology, and reproducibility guidelines.

1. Dataset Overview

StatEval consists of two complementary datasets designed to comprehensively evaluate statistical reasoning: a Foundational Knowledge Dataset covering undergraduate and graduate-level concepts, and a Statistical Research Dataset capturing advanced research-oriented tasks. Together, they span the full spectrum from fundamental probability to cutting-edge statistical theory.

Foundational Knowledge Dataset

- 6,336 undergraduate-level questions (e.g., elementary probability, linear regression, basic ML).
- 7,481 graduate-level questions (e.g., advanced probability, empirical processes, causal inference).
- Sourced from 45+ textbooks, university entrance exams, and curated course materials.

Figure 2. Composition and distribution of the Foundational Knowledge Dataset, showing proportions across subfields, educational levels, and problem formats.

Statistical Research Dataset

- 2,374 proof-based tasks (extracted from 2,719 journal papers across 18 venues).
- 8 research subdomains (e.g., classical inference, high-dimensional modeling, Bayesian nonparametrics).
- 8 theoretical property categories (e.g., asymptotic properties, optimality results, identifiability).

2. Data Processing Pipeline

The StatEval data processing pipeline is designed to balance robustness and efficiency. It integrates a multi-agent LLM workflow (with GPT-5-mini as the auxiliary model) and human-in-the-loop verification to extract and standardize problems from diverse academic sources. Supporting both PDF and LaTeX formats, the pipeline ensures structural integrity, semantic precision, and scholarly reliability throughout the entire process.

1. Format Conversion

Converts PDFs into segmentable Markdown using multimodal LLMs (e.g., MinerU), while LaTeX files are parsed directly for structured processing.

2. Sectioning & Segmentation

Documents are hierarchically segmented into meaningful sections (e.g., Examples, Theorems, Exercises) to isolate problem-containing fragments.

3. Problem Extraction

LLM agents reconstruct problems into standardized QA pairs, augmenting context when necessary for self-contained understanding and solvable reasoning tasks.

4. Standardization

All problems and solutions are normalized into structured JSON format, enabling reproducible benchmarking and consistent evaluation.

5. Classification & Labeling

Each item is assigned to either the Foundational Knowledge or Statistical Research dataset, supporting hierarchical evaluation across reasoning levels.

6. Deduplication & Human Review

Vector-based similarity checks remove duplicates, followed by expert verification to ensure conceptual distinctness and academic rigor.

Overall, the StatEval pipeline implements a full end-to-end process—from raw scholarly materials to structured, high-quality evaluation data. Its integrated design guarantees cross-corpus consistency, comprehensive coverage, and reliable benchmarking of large language models in statistical reasoning.

3. Evaluation Methodology

Evaluation for Foundational Knowledge

This section presents a hierarchical evaluation strategy for statistical problem assessment, focusing on non-proof questions primarily drawn from undergraduate and graduate textbooks, where problems are relatively clear and supported by standard answers.

Multi-Agent Evaluation Framework
1. Key Step Extractor

An LLM is employed to parse the model's responses in a structured manner, automatically extracting the core reasoning steps and essential computational results. Given the diversity of possible solution paths for different statistical problems, steps are flexibly extracted based on the actual solution to ensure coverage of the critical reasoning process.

2. Validator

After step extraction, an independent LLM validator performs a coverage check to confirm that the extracted steps encompass the main reasoning process. This mechanism prevents omission of important reasoning steps and ensures reliability and fairness in subsequent scoring.

3. Multi-dimensional Scorer

Another independent LLM evaluates the verified key steps along three complementary dimensions:

  • Reasoning Accuracy: assesses the logical validity of the reasoning process in accordance with statistical principles.
  • Step Completeness: examines whether all essential components of the solution are included without omission.
  • Final Answer Correctness: verifies the consistency of the final answer with the reference result.
Final Scoring Mechanism

To enhance robustness, multi-dimensional evaluation is performed independently three times. The final score is taken as the minimum of the three aggregated scores:

Sfinal = min{Sfinal(1), Sfinal(2), Sfinal(3)}

Where each Sfinal(i) is computed as a weighted summation:

Sfinal(i) = αSr(i) + βSs(i) + (1 - α - β)Sa(i)

With Sr(i), Ss(i), Sa(i) ∈ {0,1} representing the scores of the three dimensions in the i-th evaluation, and α, β ∈ [0,1] with α + β ≤ 1. In all experiments, we set α = 0.4 and β = 0.3.

Evaluation for Statistical Research

Evaluation of proof-based questions from research literature requires strict formal matching. Our approach employs a question rewriting – formal matching evaluation strategy to assess models' ability to handle advanced statistical reasoning and proofs.

Form Matching Evaluation for Proof-Based Questions

We remove numerical values from theoretical proof answers and rewrite theorem-based questions into standard result-oriented reasoning questions, then match the reasoning results. To ensure accurate matching, we categorize theoretical proof outcomes and apply different rules:

1. Non-constant Results

For expressions dependent on dimensions, sample sizes, or variables, the order of the dominant term (which determines the overall asymptotic order or magnitude) must match exactly to be considered correct. In cases with multiple terms, an answer is correct only if the dominant term's order strictly matches the reference, regardless of non-dominant terms. Minor differences in non-critical constant coefficients are allowed, but any deviation in the dominant term order results in an incorrect answer.

2. Constant Results

For fixed numerical values or coefficients that are decisive for the conclusion, the predicted answer must match the reference exactly.

3. Formatting Differences

Differences in formatting alone do not incur penalties and are considered correct.

4. Reproducibility Details

All benchmark resources of StatEval are publicly available to ensure transparency and reproducibility. The two datasets can be accessed via Hugging Face, while implementation details, evaluation scripts, and prompt templates are available on GitHub.
